Red Flag 02 ‚Äî Critical

ARM vs. RISC-V Decision Is Too Late

CRITICAL ‚Äî CASCADE DELAY RISK
THE PROBLEM

The plan defers the processor architecture decision to Q1‚ÄìQ2 Year 2. This is 6‚Äì12 months too late.

The entire software stack ‚Äî bootloader, BSP, DDR controller driver, OS image, and all device drivers ‚Äî depends fundamentally on this choice. If RISC-V is selected, the team faces 12‚Äì18 months of custom software development (custom bootloader, OpenSBI, DDR controller, BSP creation). Delaying this decision to mid-Year 2 makes it mathematically impossible to have the software stack ready for the Year 3 ASIC tape-out.

‚ö† Impact Analysis

If ARM: Mature ecosystem, U-Boot and Linux work out of the box. 3‚Äì6 months of porting. Decision delay is costly but survivable.

If RISC-V: Custom bootloader, OpenSBI, DDR controller, full BSP from scratch. 12‚Äì18 months minimum. A Year 2 decision leaves zero margin for Year 3 tape-out.

‚úì Recommendation

Lock this decision by Q2 Year 1. Define evaluation framework in Month 1‚Äì2. Use QEMU to prototype both architectures in parallel during Q1‚ÄìQ2, then commit based on measured engineering data (boot time, driver maturity, BSP effort) ‚Äî not speculation.

Red Flag 03 ‚Äî Critical

RF ASIC in Year 4 Is Extremely Aggressive

CRITICAL ‚Äî SCHEDULE FAILURE RISK
THE PROBLEM

Designing a custom RF front-end ASIC (PA, LNA, filters, switches, matching networks) in a single year is unrealistic.

RF ASIC design requires extensive EM simulation, layout-dependent parasitic extraction, silicon characterization, and typically multiple iterations. Industry timelines are 18‚Äì24 months. The plan allocates 12 months and assumes first silicon success.

‚ö† Specific Concerns

PA design alone typically requires 6‚Äì9 months. Combined with LNA, filters, switches, and matching networks, this is a full standalone project. First silicon almost never works perfectly for RF ‚Äî one respin is the norm, adding 4‚Äì6 months.

‚úì Recommendation

Start RF ASIC architecture in early Year 3. Budget for one respin. Consider whether a custom RF ASIC is truly necessary ‚Äî an RF module or SiP approach may deliver 80% of the benefit at 20% of the risk. Plan Year 4 pilot production with a hybrid fallback: custom SoC + COTS RF front-end.

Red Flag 04 ‚Äî High

No Verification Strategy Detail

HIGH ‚Äî SILICON QUALITY RISK
THE PROBLEM

ASIC verification typically consumes 60‚Äì70% of digital design effort. The plan mentions it in one bullet point.

For a multi-block SoC integrating MCU, DSP, and RF transceiver, the verification challenge is enormous. No methodology, coverage targets, team allocation, or VIP budget is specified.

‚ö† What's Missing

No verification methodology (UVM, formal, emulation). No coverage targets (functional, code, assertion). No dedicated verification team ‚Äî the 15‚Äì20 digital designers are expected to verify their own blocks. No VIP for AXI, DDR, SPI interfaces. No system-level verification strategy.

‚úì Recommendation

Create a dedicated verification sub-plan by end of Year 1. Allocate 50%+ of digital team (8‚Äì10 engineers) to verification. Define UVM testbench architecture. Set coverage targets (95% functional, 90% code). Budget for VIP. Plan system-level verification via FPGA emulation in Year 2.

Red Flag 05 ‚Äî High

No ASIC Failure Contingency

HIGH ‚Äî PROJECT CONTINUITY RISK
THE PROBLEM

What happens if the Year 3 SoC tape-out fails? The plan has no fallback.

First-time silicon success rates for complex SoCs are not 100%. Metal respin, logic bugs, integration issues, or foundry defects can cause partial or total failure.

‚ö† Failure Scenarios

Metal-fix respin: 3‚Äì4 months + $200‚Äì400K. Full respin: 6‚Äì9 months + $600K‚Äì1M. Fundamental issue: Return to FPGA stage. None addressed in budget or timeline.

‚úì Recommendation

Maintain FPGA-based production capability through Year 3 silicon validation. Budget for at least one metal respin. Define go/no-go criteria for FPGA ‚Üí ASIC transition. Build 3-month schedule buffer.

Red Flag 06 ‚Äî High

No Process Node Selection

HIGH ‚Äî COST & FEASIBILITY RISK
THE PROBLEM

The choice of fabrication process (28nm? 65nm? 130nm?) has massive implications for everything.

This affects tape-out cost ($500K at 130nm vs. $3M+ at 28nm), IP libraries, power consumption, EDA requirements, and foundry lead times. Without this decision, the team cannot finalize budget, select IP, or plan foundry engagement.

‚ö† Cascading Impact

Cannot finalize tape-out budget. Cannot select ARM core IP or DDR PHY. Cannot estimate power for drone platform. Cannot determine if $1.2‚Äì1.5M tape-out budget is realistic.

‚úì Recommendation

Year 1 Q1‚ÄìQ2 decision. For a drone datalink SoC, 65nm or 40nm is the sweet spot ‚Äî balancing cost, power, and IP availability. Commission a process node trade study as one of the first Year 1 activities.

Red Flag 07 ‚Äî High

Security Is Underspecified

HIGH ‚Äî MISSION-CRITICAL GAP
THE PROBLEM

For a drone datalink, security is mission-critical. The plan mentions it once in Year 2.

A wireless communication system for drones must defend against interception, jamming, spoofing, and replay attacks. The plan treats security as a feature rather than a foundational architecture requirement.

‚ö† What's Missing

No threat model. No anti-jamming / frequency hopping spec. No COMSEC architecture. No hardware root of trust. No secure key provisioning plan. No penetration testing phase. Security cannot be bolted on ‚Äî it must be designed in from Day 1.

‚úì Recommendation

Add a dedicated security workstream from Year 1 Q1. Threat modeling in Month 1‚Äì2. Hardware root of trust and crypto requirements before architecture freeze. Anti-jamming as a first-class RF requirement. Security audit before tape-out. Penetration testing in Year 4.

8‚Äì12 SW engineers building a full Linux BSP, codec stack, and modem firmware with no automated testing.

Structural Suggestions

Improvements to Plan Structure

Beyond specific risks, the plan needs these structural additions for governance and stakeholder confidence.

üö¶ Gate Reviews Between Phases

Each phase transition needs defined exit criteria, a formal review board, and documented evidence that milestones are met. This is standard in aerospace/defense programs and gives management clear intervention points.

üìä Technology Readiness Level (TRL)

Map each phase to TRL 1‚Äì9. Phase 1 = TRL 3‚Äì4, Phase 2 = TRL 5, Phase 3 = TRL 6‚Äì7, Phase 4 = TRL 8‚Äì9. Gives stakeholders a standardized way to understand progress.

ü§ù Formalize Multi-Party Collaboration

The company-university-third party collaboration must be in the main plan body. Define IP ownership, RACI matrix, coordination overhead, and data sharing agreements. This can make or break the project.

‚ö° Power Budget & Link Budget

For a drone system, power is a hard constraint. Define a formal power budget in Year 1 and track as a key metric alongside an RF link budget through all phases.

üñ•Ô∏è Virtual Platform / MBD Workstream

No mention of QEMU or Model-Based Design. Critical for enabling SW dev from Month 1, de-risking ARM/RISC-V, and CI/CD. See the QEMU Virtual Platform Proposal for full details.

üìù Risk Register with Owners

No formal risk register exists. Each risk needs an owner, probability/impact rating, mitigation plan, and escalation trigger. The PM team should own this as a living document reviewed at each gate.
